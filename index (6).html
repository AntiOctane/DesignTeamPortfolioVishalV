Solar Gators
Battery Management System Tester PCB
The Problem

One major problem our team came into was testing Battery Management System Boards, as many times we would end up accidentally damaging expensive production boards. Furthermore, unbalanced LiPo cell batteries are very dangerous to test on, so I designed this PCB so that we can safely test cell monitoring charging and discharging of our Battery Management Systems without damaging boards or causing a LiPo fire hazard.

What I Did
  • Used the TPS55010, an fly buck converter to get an isolated output ground, then chained six of them so their grounds stack like LiPo cells (2.5 to 4.2 V each).
  • Implemented an AD5241BRZ10k digital potentiometer to adjust the feedback voltage of our fly buck converter enabling voltage control over a range of 2.5-4.2V allowing stress testing BMS over a variety of cell ranges.
  • Ran compensation, transformer, feedback resistor, and diode stress calculations from the datasheet first, then validated under 20 mV ripple at 200 mA across all cells over a 2.5-4.2V range in PSpice simulation prior to routing.
  • Routed 4-layer PCB by keeping I2C traces on the left side of board, switching nodes on the right. Made switching node loop area short and isolated from switching frequency selector, feedback resistor and compensation. Implemented 6 isolated ground planes and for each junction had a via
  • Added an RC snubber to kill inductive spikes from the converter on the secondary side, plus a separate 3.3 V buck rail for the STM32 and digital pots.
Outcome

Delivered a complete, fabrication-ready 4-layer design that lets the team safely exercise our BMS across the full 2.5–4.2 V per-cell range without risking production boards or a LiPo fire hazard. Validated under 20 mV ripple at 200 mA across all six cells in PSpice, with compensation, transformer, feedback, and diode stress calculations worked from the datasheets before routing. The board has been handed off to the organization for fabrication and use as a standing test tool. The biggest takeaway was understanding why each component and value is chosen rather than copying the reference design as understanding the design in depth first caught issues that wouldn't have shown up until hardware. Re-reviewing the layout, I also identified routing optimizations (tighter switching-node loops, cleaner I2C/feedback separation) for a future revision.  

SASE Engineering Team
Search and Rescue Robots
The Problem

Our design teams' goal is to design prototype search and rescue robots to perform delivery aid and find other injured civilians in search and response. As an electrical member in my first year, and the electrical lead in my second year, my goal was to lead and design the electrical system of prototype search and rescue robots.

What I Did
  • Quadruped: Simulated gait in MATLAB to get joint angles, then implemented 45 kg/cm servos with a 2.5x factor of safety. The 12 servo system peaks at 60 A, so we used a 2S LiPo that balanced handling large current and weight so the dog could walk.
  • Used a star ground topology implementing a power distribution PCB board with separate buck/boost converters for each subsystem primarily sensors, Jetson Orin Nano, and motor drivers.
  • Implemented an LC filter on the Jetson rail for EMI mitigation, TVS diode to clamp voltage spikes, and optocoupler isolation on the motor drivers to protect microcontroller.
  • Collected IMU, encoder, and motor PWM data while driving on gravel, identifying main noise frequencies and built a 4th-order low-pass in MATLAB at 10 Hz cutoff and -80 dB per decade, ending up with 312x noise rejection for unwanted frequencies. This caused some phase lag however, so we traded speed for accuracy
  • For sensor selection I chose the BMI270 over SPI for fast orientation data, RPLiDAR A1 for mapping, and hall effect encoders with a simple 1st-order filter for positional feedback. To integrate different sensor sampling rates into 1kHz control loop, I wrote embedded C firmware with hardware interrupts and DMA reducing latency.
Outcome

Delivered two working platforms. The quadruped walks under its own power and streams live IMU telemetry; the treaded robot completes 60 second autonomous navigation runs on gravel at 0.3 m/s with under 7 cm positional error. As electrical lead I designed the power and sensing architecture and integrated it with mechanical and software design constraints. While the robot worked, the runs showed some improvements that should be made for the next iteration,primarily tighter wiring harnessing and cable management for modularity and easier debugging, and debris shielding for longer terrain runs which were both consequences of mid project mechanical/electrical scope changes that I should have finalized earlier with clearer team communication.
